This Framework is submitted in support of HRTO Cases 2026-65656-I and 2025-63706-I as a formal accommodation framework and proposed systemic remedy under s. 45.2(1)(3) of the Ontario Human Rights Code. It is grounded exclusively in verified Ontario and Canadian law, clinical evidence, and binding judicial authority. It is offered not only in resolution of the individual cases before the Tribunal, but as a principled standard for the entire Ontario education system — the first of its kind in Canada.
§ 0
Factual Narrative: The Applicant's Experience
0.1 — Background: The Applicant
0.1.1 Nicholas Desjardins is a mature student with a clinically documented 43-point neurological processing gap established at SickKids Hospital in 1994 (Clinical Record #1211670). He brings to his studies over 20 years of professional experience as a Master Carpenter — a career requiring precision, sustained attention, complex problem-solving, and technical mastery. His disability does not reflect a lack of capacity or intelligence. It reflects a documented neurological difference in how information is processed under institutional conditions that are not designed for his cognitive profile.
0.1.2 The Applicant has identified AI assistive tools as the accommodation that bridges his documented processing gap. These tools allow him to access, organize, and produce academic work at a level commensurate with his actual knowledge and capability. Without them, institutional barriers — not his ability — determine his outcomes.
0.2 — HRTO Case 2025-63706-I: TVO Independent Learning Centre
0.2.1 The Applicant enrolled in ENG4U at TVO Independent Learning Centre (TVO ILC). In fulfillment of Assignment 1.5 — "To be resilient, or not to be resilient" — he produced a formal essay on the subject of Hamlet (Course: ENG4U-EN-02-02-ON-(I-D-0922), Student ID: 99516014). This work represented approximately 500 hours of sustained effort across multiple submissions — a volume of investment consistent with his 20-year professional work ethic as a Master Carpenter and directly reflective of his commitment to academic achievement.
0.2.2 On September 18, 2025, the Applicant submitted Assignment 1.5. That submission was processed through TVO ILC's own in-house Turnitin portal, which returned a confirmed result of 0% AI-detected content (Turnitin Submission ID: 2755196691). TVO ILC's own published policy states that scores between 0% and 24% are within acceptable parameters. The Applicant's submission fell entirely within that range.
0.2.3 Despite this 0% result, TVO ILC's Academic Officer Jane subsequently produced a conflicting internal score of 44% AI detection, attributed to an earlier draft submission (Submission ID: 122804108). In written correspondence dated September 29, 2025, Jane stated: "The Turn It In software has indicated 44% of the reflection piece to contain unoriginal material. Please note, Turnitin has an accuracy rate of 98% especially with assignments that come back at high percentages." No explanation was provided for the 20-point discrepancy between the institution's own acceptable threshold of 24% and the 44% score, nor for the conflict between the 44% score on one submission and the 0% score on another. No mark was assigned. This constitutes a procedurally deficient adverse action under Bill 149 (Working for Workers Act, 2023) — the institution failed to disclose the specific algorithmic parameters used and failed to reconcile conflicting outputs before taking adverse action.
0.2.4 When the Applicant communicated his disability and accommodation needs, TVO ILC's written response on October 7, 2025 stated that accommodations at TVO ILC are "limited to the final test" only — expressly excluding assignments. The institution further advised the Applicant to "reach out to a friend or family member" for academic support. This response constitutes a blanket denial of accommodation for assignments — the primary vehicle of academic assessment — and an inadequate, non-individualized substitute that fails the duty to accommodate under s. 17 OHRC.
0.2.5 The institution's own email correspondence of October 7, 2025 was flagged by external security systems as a high-risk phishing attack, displaying the warning: "This message might be dangerous — Messages like these ones were used to steal personal information." Simultaneously, the Applicant's work product was transmitted without consent to Turnitin LLC, an American corporation headquartered in Oakland, California, subjecting his personal health information contained in his accommodation plan to U.S. federal surveillance law including the USA PATRIOT Act. This constitutes a cross-border data breach and a failure of the institution's obligations under PIPEDA and MFIPPA to protect student data with reasonable safeguards.
0.2.6 On October 8, 2025, the Applicant formally notified TVO ILC of these violations and demanded resolution within three business days. No legally compliant written response was received. The Applicant subsequently filed HRTO Case 2025-63706-I. The institution's conduct — refusing to mark work that passed its own detection standard, limiting accommodations to final tests only, and transmitting health information cross-border without consent — represents a complete institutional failure of the duty to accommodate a student with a documented disability.
0.3 — HRTO Case 2026-65656-I: Sheridan College
0.3.1 The Applicant enrolled at Sheridan College (Student ID: 000804753) in the School of Applied Computing. The institution constructed a course schedule spanning seven concurrent classes, cycling from early morning to late evening, Monday through Friday, with breaks of approximately ten minutes between sessions. The Applicant describes this as a structure that delivered over 5,000 data points per week — a volume that is humanly impossible to retain and process under any circumstances, but acutely and disproportionately harmful to a student with a documented 43-point neurological processing gap.
0.3.2 This scheduling structure directly caused clinical sensory exhaustion and tinnitus-induced neural overload. The Applicant's disability renders him significantly more vulnerable to cognitive overload under conditions of sustained environmental and informational pressure. The institution deployed AI-facilitated lectures and binary assessment modules that were medically incompatible with his documented cognitive processing profile. The institutional environment constituted a systemic barrier within the meaning of Meiorin, [1999] 3 SCR 3.
0.3.2(a) Selective Denial of Clinical Record. Sheridan College's Accessible Learning office, in communications confirmed by Interim Associate Dean Jennifer Gillespie during a recorded meeting, refused to recognize the Applicant's SickKids Clinical Record #1211670 in its entirety. Specifically, the institution declined to accommodate the Applicant's documented ADHD, dyslexia, and 43-point neurological processing gap — recognizing only the tinnitus diagnosis for the purpose of granting minimal additional test time. Accessible Learning advisor Nicholas Matree's written communication to the Applicant explicitly confirmed this selective denial by reference to DVF Form 8. In the recorded meeting, Interim Associate Dean Gillespie confirmed the institution's position as: "based on your current documented disability" — expressly excluding the full clinical picture established at SickKids in 1994. The institution's refusal to recognize a clinically established, multi-decade disability record from a nationally recognized pediatric hospital, in favour of a narrow administrative classification, constitutes a failure of the individualized assessment obligation under s. 17 OHRC and McGill University Health Centre v. Syndicat, [2007] 1 SCR 161. An institution is not entitled to re-litigate or selectively accept clinical findings from qualified specialists.
0.3.3 On February 2, 2026, the Applicant formally notified the Office of General Counsel and Associate Dean Mark Orlando that the institution had failed to implement required accommodations and that the environment was causing active medical harm. He requested an immediate transition to remote self-directed learning with AI assistive tools — a no-cost accommodation that would have allowed him to continue his studies from home, processing curriculum at a medically appropriate pace. This request was grounded in his SickKids clinical record and HRTO File No. 2025-63706-I already on record.
0.3.4 Associate Dean Mark Orlando's written response dated February 4, 2026 did not engage with the medical or accommodation substance of the Applicant's notice. Instead, it reframed a documented medical and human rights crisis as a "communication expectations" issue, providing a departmental routing chart and warning of potential escalation for "disruptive or concerning behaviour." This response is documented as a failure to engage in the good-faith individualized accommodation assessment required under OHRC s. 17 and Hydro-Québec, [2008] 2 SCR 561.
0.3.5 Interim Associate Dean Jennifer Gillespie's written response dated February 9, 2026 confirmed that the modality of the program was "not changing," that there was "no requirement for faculty to provide 48 hours' notice for assessments," and that the Applicant's option was to "consider reducing your course load." This response constitutes a formal institutional refusal to accommodate. Reducing course load is not an equivalent accommodation — it is a diminishment of the Applicant's educational access. The institution's failure to engage with the specific accommodation requested, in favour of a lesser administrative suggestion, breaches the duty to accommodate to the point of undue hardship under s. 17(2) OHRC.
0.3.6 As a direct result of the institution's failure to accommodate, the Applicant was forced to take medical leave. A 20-year construction veteran who returned to education as a vital career transition was removed from his program — not by lack of ability or effort, but by an institution that designed an environment incompatible with his disability and refused every request to modify it. This outcome is precisely the harm the Ontario Human Rights Code was designed to prevent.
0.4 — The Systemic Pattern
0.4.1 These two cases, taken together, reveal a systemic pattern: institutions that simultaneously refuse the human supports they are obligated to provide, refuse the technological accommodations that would serve as an adequate alternative, and then apply academic integrity tools in a manner that penalizes the very accommodation the student requires. The result is a system that is structurally inaccessible to students with cognitive processing disabilities — not through malice, but through institutional design that has never been required to account for these needs.
0.4.2 This is why the Applicant brings this matter not only as an individual claim but as a vehicle for systemic change. The HRTO has the authority under s. 45.2(1)(3) OHRC to order remedies that address the root of the discrimination, not merely its symptoms in a single case. That authority is invoked here, in full.
§ 1
Definitions and Interpretive Principles
"AI Assistive Tool"
Any artificial intelligence application used by a student with a documented disability to access, process, produce, or communicate curriculum content, where such use is prescribed or supported by clinical evidence. Analogous in legal character to corrective lenses, hearing aids, or mobility devices under OHRC s. 10(1).
"Processing Gap"
A clinically documented neurological difference affecting a student's ability to access curriculum through standard means, as established by qualified clinical assessment. Reference: SickKids Clinical Record #1211670, documenting a 43-point processing differential.
"Duty to Accommodate"
The obligation under OHRC s. 17 to take all steps to meet the needs of a person with a disability short of undue hardship, assessed with reference to cost, outside sources of funding, and health and safety.
"Systemic Barrier"
An institutional policy, practice, or technology deployment that disproportionately disadvantages persons with cognitive or learning disabilities, regardless of discriminatory intent. See Meiorin, [1999] 3 SCR 3.
"AI Detection Tool"
Any algorithmic system (e.g., Turnitin AI, GPTZero) purporting to identify AI-generated content, which has been documented to produce unreliable results against neurodiverse output. See IPC–OHRC Joint Principles for the Responsible Use of Artificial Intelligence, January 21, 2026.
§ 2
Constitutional and Statutory Foundation
2.1 — Quasi-Constitutional Primacy of the Human Rights Code
2.1.1 The Ontario Human Rights Code is quasi-constitutional in character. Pursuant to s. 47(2) OHRC, where a provision of any other Act conflicts with the Code, the Code prevails. All institutional AI policies, academic integrity handbooks, and acceptable-use policies are therefore subordinate to the accommodation obligations established herein. (Insurance Corp. of British Columbia v. Heerspink, [1982] 2 SCR 145)
2.1.2 The Supreme Court of Canada has confirmed that human rights legislation must be given a large and liberal interpretation consistent with its remedial purpose. (Ontario Human Rights Commission v. Simpsons-Sears Ltd., [1985] 2 SCR 536 — "O'Malley")
2.2 — Section 15 Charter: Equality Rights
2.2.1 Section 15(1) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms guarantees equal benefit of the law without discrimination based on mental or physical disability. Applying AI-detection software — proven to produce disparate false-positive rates against neurodivergent output — constitutes a s. 15 infringement requiring s. 1 Oakes justification.
2.3 — Section 7 Charter: Security of the Person
2.3.1 Removal of a clinically validated assistive technology that a student with a documented processing disability relies upon for cognitive access engages s. 7 Charter security of the person. The deprivation is not in accordance with principles of fundamental justice where no adequate substitute is provided. (Blencoe v. British Columbia (Human Rights Commission), [2000] 2 SCR 307)
§ 3
AI as a Remedial Assistive Device
"The prohibition on discrimination requires that persons with disabilities be accommodated in ways that meet their needs, not merely in ways that are convenient for the institution."
— Eldridge v. British Columbia (Attorney General), [1997] 3 SCR 624
3.1 The Eyeglass Principle. Under s. 10(1) OHRC, a "disability" includes any degree of physical or mental disability. Assistive devices that remediate the functional impact of a disability — corrective lenses, hearing aids, mobility aids — are protected accommodations. An AI tool that bridges a clinically documented processing gap is legally analogous: its removal is a withdrawal of accommodation, not a policy enforcement.
3.2 Clinical Validation. SickKids Clinical Record #1211670 documents a 43-point processing differential established in 1994. This constitutes objective clinical evidence of a disability within the meaning of s. 10(1) OHRC. The institution is not entitled to re-litigate this clinical finding through its own assessment processes.
3.3 Individualized Assessment Required. The Supreme Court has established that accommodation must be individualized to the specific needs of the person. A blanket institutional AI ban, applied without regard to disability-related need, fails the individualized assessment requirement. (McGill University Health Centre (Montreal General Hospital) v. Syndicat des employés de l'Hôpital général de Montréal, [2007] 1 SCR 161)
Binding Judicial Authority — The Bokhari Standard
In Bokhari v. Top Medical Transportation Services Inc., 2026 ONSC 1073, the Ontario Divisional Court confirmed that the HRTO cannot engage in merits-based screening at the jurisdictional stage that requires the applicant to characterize the nature of their disability. The Court held that where institutional policy is itself the discriminatory mechanism, the Tribunal must examine the policy — not the claimant's medical file — at the threshold. This directly governs HRTO Cases 2026-65656-I and 2025-63706-I: the question before the Tribunal is not whether the applicant's processing gap meets a severity threshold, but whether the institution's AI ban is itself a socio-political barrier to access.
§ 4
The Duty to Accommodate to the Point of Undue Hardship
4.1 The Standard. Under s. 17(2) OHRC, an institution must accommodate to the point of undue hardship, having regard only to cost, outside sources of funding, and health and safety. Administrative inconvenience, institutional preference, and policy uniformity are not factors.
4.2 The Undue Hardship Threshold. The Supreme Court set a high threshold for undue hardship. The accommodation need not be perfect, but must be the best possible accommodation in all the circumstances. (Central Okanagan School District No. 23 v. Renaud, [1992] 2 SCR 970)
4.3 No-Cost Accommodation. Where a proposed accommodation imposes no direct cost on the institution — as is the case with a student using a personally owned AI tool — the institution's ability to invoke undue hardship is severely constrained. The accommodation requested here requires no institutional expenditure whatsoever: the student supplies the tool, the device, and the access. iA Financial Group's $1,000,000 donation to the SickKids AI (SKAI) program (March 19, 2026) further evidences the broader institutional recognition that AI integration in education and health is a funded, responsible priority — not an experimental or cost-prohibitive outlier.
4.4 Systemic Failure as Context. The May 12, 2026 Auditor General Special Report documents that Educational Assistant vacancies go unfilled up to 72% of the time and that 33% of students with special needs wait over one year for required assessments. Where documented systemic failures prevent the institution from providing mandated human supports, alternative accommodations — including AI assistive tools — become mandatory. A system that refuses the human alternative and the technological alternative simultaneously has abandoned its legal duty entirely.
4.5 The Accommodation Process. The duty to accommodate is a procedural as well as substantive obligation. The institution must engage in a genuine, good-faith individualized assessment of accommodation options. (Hydro-Québec v. Syndicat des employé-e-s de techniques professionnelles et de bureau d'Hydro-Québec, section locale 2000 (SCFP-FTQ), [2008] 2 SCR 561)
§ 5
Prohibition on AI Detection Tools Against Accommodated Students
5.1 Discriminatory Effect. AI detection tools (Turnitin AI, GPTZero, and equivalents) applied to the work of a student using an AI assistive tool as an accommodation constitute prima facie discrimination under s. 1 OHRC. The discriminatory effect arises not from intent but from the disparate impact on a student whose accommodation is the very output the tool flags.
5.2 Reliability Standard. The IPC–OHRC Joint Principles for the Responsible Use of Artificial Intelligence (January 21, 2026) establish that AI systems used in institutional contexts must be valid, reliable, human-rights affirming, and transparent. Applied to AI detection tools (Turnitin AI, GPTZero, and equivalents), these principles are violated where such tools produce contradictory outputs — as documented in this record (0% vs. 44% on the same student's work) — and where the institution fails to disclose the algorithmic parameters employed. Under Bill 149 (Working for Workers Act, 2023), any entity using AI to screen or assess output must provide written disclosure of the algorithmic parameters employed. Failure to disclose renders any adverse action based on that output procedurally deficient.
5.3 Reprisal Protection. Any adverse academic or administrative action taken against a student following that student's assertion of accommodation rights constitutes reprisal under s. 8 OHRC. This includes "integrity interviews," grade penalties, or increased scrutiny temporally connected to the exercise of rights. (Noble v. York University, 2010 HRTO 878)
5.4 The Cell Phone Corollary. On April 28, 2026, Ontario Education Minister Paul Calandra publicly stated that any provincial cell phone restriction would contain medical exemptions, confirming that devices used for health and special education needs are exempt from general prohibition. A student's mobile device configured as a primary interface for an AI assistive tool that is part of an accommodation plan falls squarely within that medical and special education exemption and is not subject to general device restrictions.
§ 6
Privacy, Data Sovereignty, and Academic Integrity
6.1 Privacy by Design. Under the Joint Resolution of Canadian Privacy Commissioners (2023) and Bill 194 (Strengthening Cyber Security and Building Trust Act, Ontario 2024), institutions must implement privacy-by-design principles. A student's AI-assisted drafts, prompts, and cognitive process materials are sensitive personal health information and may not be harvested for institutional analytics or third-party AI training.
6.2 Data Residency. Pursuant to O. Reg. 51/26 (EDSTA), effective July 1, 2026, institutions are required to ensure digital security standards are met. Submitting a student's accommodated work product to foreign-hosted AI detection servers without consent violates applicable privacy obligations and the student's accommodation. The IPC's jurisdictional declination in NJ25-00506 (January 6, 2026) confirms that Ontario's provincial privacy framework does not extend to private foreign platforms such as Turnitin LLC — a structural gap that makes the institution, as the transmitting party, the locus of accountability under MFIPPA and PIPEDA.
6.3 Provenance and Attribution. Under IEEE Standard 2890-2025 for provenance of data, and consistent with the student's authorship rights under the Copyright Act, RSC 1985, c C-42, cognitive output produced with AI assistance by a student with a documented accommodation retains the student's authorship. Flagging such output as "AI-generated" without regard for the accommodation context is a misattribution that prejudices the student's rights.
6.4 Cyber Security Incident Designation. Pursuant to O. Reg. 51/26 (EDSTA), effective July 1, 2026, any unilateral lockout or suspension of a student's AI assistive tool must be reported to the Ministry as a Critical Cyber Security Incident within 72 hours where that tool constitutes an accommodation under an active IEP or accommodation plan.
§ 7
Proposed Systemic Remedies

Pursuant to s. 45.2(1)(3) OHRC, the HRTO may order a public institution to implement systemic remedies where discrimination is found to be organizational or policy-based. The following remedies are proposed as proportionate, evidence-based, and in the public interest.

Systemic Remedy 1 — Formal Recognition
AI Assistive Tool Policy. The Respondent institution shall adopt a formal written policy recognizing AI tools as eligible assistive technology accommodations under the OHRC for students with documented processing or learning disabilities, assessed on an individualized basis consistent with the student's IEP or clinical documentation.
Systemic Remedy 2 — Detection Tool Prohibition
Prohibition on AI Detection Against Accommodated Students. The Respondent shall prohibit the use of AI detection software against the work product of any student whose accommodation plan includes AI assistive tools, absent the student's written, informed consent and a validated human review process.
Systemic Remedy 3 — Staff Training
Mandatory Training. The Respondent shall provide mandatory training to all academic and administrative staff on: (a) the legal obligation to accommodate AI assistive tools; (b) the unreliability of AI detection software; and (c) reprisal prohibition under s. 8 OHRC.
Systemic Remedy 4 — Continuity of Accommodation
No Unilateral Withdrawal. Once an AI assistive tool is integrated into a student's accommodation plan, it may not be unilaterally removed without: (a) a good-faith individualized assessment; (b) written notice to the student; and (c) provision of an equivalent alternative accommodation meeting the student's documented needs.
Systemic Remedy 5 — Provincial Standard
Ministry of Education Direction. The Tribunal is requested to direct the Ontario Ministry of Education to issue provincial guidance establishing AI assistive tools as a recognized accommodation category within Ontario's Special Education framework, consistent with the Education Act, RSO 1990 and the OHRC.

§ 10
Closing Statement: The Case for Systemic Change

This Framework presents a simple proposition: a student with a documented neurological processing gap of 43 points, whose institution has simultaneously failed to provide the mandated human supports that gap requires and prohibited the technological accommodation that would bridge it, has been denied equal access to education in violation of the Ontario Human Rights Code.

The law is clear. The evidence is documented by the Auditor General, the IPC and Ontario Human Rights Commission jointly, and the Courts. The accommodation is available at no institutional cost. What is missing is the systemic recognition that AI tools, when prescribed by clinical need and used as assistive technology, are entitled to the same protection as any other accommodation device.

The HRTO has the authority under s. 45.2(1)(3) OHRC to order systemic remedies that extend beyond the individual applicant. Ontario can be the first jurisdiction in Canada to establish AI as a recognized and protected assistive technology in education. That is not a radical proposition. It is the natural extension of forty years of human rights jurisprudence applied to a new category of tool.

The Desjardins Framework is offered to the Tribunal as that standard.
